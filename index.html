01Qualitative Research · Focus Group · Consumer Psychology

Consumer Behavior Study – Cooking Appliance Purchase Decisions

✨ Key Outcome: Through a multi-stage research design, clarified the sources of decision anxiety among different user groups in high-choice contexts, helping the client optimize product positioning and communication strategy.

This study focused on how users evaluate and purchase cooking appliances. A three-stage design was adopted: participant grouping → pre-interview homework → qualitative interviews — ensuring sufficient user background context was established before interviews, so insights better reflected real behavior rather than answers constructed on the spot.

Research Design — Three-Stage Process

  • Participant Segmentation (8 people / 4 groups): Stratified by age, parental status, cooking frequency, existing kitchen appliances, purchase intent within 6 months, and monthly income. Ensured each group shared similar life contexts while capturing behavioral differences across groups.
  • Pre-Interview Homework: Participants completed questionnaires or lifestyle journals before interviews, recording daily cooking habits, current appliance usage, and purchase considerations — activating their real experience so responses were more concrete and authentic.
  • Qualitative Interviews: Deep-dived into motivations and emotions behind the homework, explored points of hesitation in the purchase decision flow, and examined how brand trust is established.

Key Insights

  • Too many options created decision anxiety — users need clear comparison criteria, not more features.
  • Trust-building takes priority over feature count, arising from word-of-mouth, brand familiarity, and information consistency.
  • Decision considerations varied significantly across segments (with/without children, high/low cooking frequency), making a single communication strategy insufficient to reach all groups.

Learnings

  • Pre-interview homework noticeably improved interview quality — participants arrived with concrete experiences, making insights more grounded in real behavior.
  • Research design itself is part of research quality; grouping logic directly affects the depth and credibility of insights.
Impact: Helped clarify decision factors across different user segments, providing concrete direction for product positioning, marketing messaging, and user communication.

02Stakeholder Interview · Information Architecture · Website PM

MAPEX Global Website Redesign (KHS)

✨ Key Outcome: Helped the brand upgrade from an outdated static website to a global site with clear product pathways and strong visual brand identity — now officially live.

MAPEX is a globally recognized drum brand. The original website had an outdated module architecture, lacking visual flexibility and interactive effects, unable to support the brand's upscale positioning or extensive product line. The client's core need was for drummers and instrument enthusiasts to intuitively find the right products, while strengthening the brand's premium visual identity.

My Role

  • Conducted requirements interviews with the client's PM and marketing team, clarifying brand objectives, product line architecture, and target user profiles.
  • Translated the client's description of "more stylish, more complete product pathways" into concrete page logic and product categorization structures.
  • Collaborated with engineers to confirm module selection, page flow, and development priorities.
  • Coordinated communication between client-side and development-side throughout, ensuring requirements weren't distorted in transmission.

UX Thinking

  • Designed product navigation around "how users find the right drum kit" — not based on internal product codes.
  • Simplified navigation hierarchy to reduce the sense of disorientation when browsing multiple product lines (reduced cognitive load).
  • Aligned visual direction with brand positioning to strengthen first-impression trust and professionalism.
  • Built an extensible content structure to support future product line expansion and multi-region content additions.
Result: Website is officially live → mapexdrums.com ↗ — Post-redesign product pathways are clearer, and the visual presentation meets client expectations for brand feel.

03Self-Initiated · Qualitative Research · Consumer Behavior

E-commerce Platform Purchase Decision Behavior Study

✨ Key Outcome: Discovered significant differences in platform selection and decision-making patterns by gender, age, and income — proposed design directions to reduce decision costs.

This was a self-initiated qualitative study, interviewing people from my social circle. Participants were deliberately chosen across different genders, occupations, marital statuses, and monthly incomes. The goal was to explore users' decision-making flow on e-commerce platforms from search to purchase, identifying key variables that influence behavior. This was an exploratory study, focused on pattern discovery and hypothesis generation rather than statistical validation.

Research Method

  • User Interviews: Understanding pre-purchase thought processes, hesitation points, and sources of trust.
  • Behavioral Observation: Observing how users search, filter, and compare products.
  • Qualitative Analysis: Organizing recurring behavioral patterns and decision factors, then performing segmented analysis by demographic variables.

Key Insights

  • Gender differences in decision-making: Men tended to gather specific technical specs before purchasing; women relied more on brand familiarity and word-of-mouth, rarely comparing numbers item-by-item — trust took priority over feature comparison.
  • Age differences in platform choice: Users 30+ preferred Amazon, Momo, or brand official sites, prioritizing stability and after-sales assurance. Those in their early 20s preferred Shopee, accepting uncertainty in exchange for low prices, with high sensitivity to "deals."
  • Design implications: Data-driven users need clear spec comparison features; trust-driven users need stronger review systems and brand certification.

Learnings

  • The limitation of small-sample qualitative research is the inability to make statistical generalizations — but it can quickly surface hypotheses and behavioral patterns.
  • The platform's trust-mechanism design influences whether users complete a purchase more than the product itself.
Expected Impact: Designing differentiated information presentation and trust mechanisms by user segment can effectively reduce decision time and improve conversion rates.
